01 — The Problem Worth Solving
Millions of people in Latin America are metabolically at risk.
Most don't know it.

The gap isn't the technology. It's the pathway — no one has built the habit of metabolic self-awareness into places where people actually go.

Diabetes, hypertension, and dyslipidemia are Mexico's leading chronic disease burden. Combined, they account for over 35% of national mortality. The detection infrastructure today requires a central lab, a doctor's referral, a 12-hour fast, and results in 48 hours. For middle-market Mexico, every one of those steps is a reason to defer.

This isn't a clinical problem. It's a behavioral access problem wearing a clinical mask. The question isn't "how do we build a better test?" — it's "how do we make knowing your metabolic risk as normal as checking your blood pressure at a pharmacy?"

The behavioral hypothesis is wrong.

Patients get a quantitative result and don't act on it. If <10% book a follow-up within 14 days across both pilot sites, the access problem isn't what I think it is. We'd be building a curiosity tool, not a health intervention.

The Brazil-first pathway depends on Class II Notificação. If the product gets classified as Class III, the 9–12 month dual-market timeline collapses. Week-one RA consultant call is non-negotiable.

What I do about each

Each of these has a specific 90-day check built into the validation sprint. The pilot isn't just about proving the product works. It's about finding out which of these assumptions is wrong before we scale.

The one I'm most confident about

The behavioral hypothesis. Not because I know it's right — but because the TuDoc model was specifically designed around it. If any environment can prove behavior change, it's a closed membership loop with a teleconsult built in.

Appendix A — Tech Reference
Technology reference.
How a lateral flow test works

A lateral flow assay (LFA) is a paper-based platform that detects a target analyte in a sample. Blood, urine, or saliva is applied to a membrane and migrates via capillary action through: a conjugate pad (antibodies tagged with colored particles), a nitrocellulose membrane with test and control lines, and an absorbent pad. If the target molecule is present, it binds at the test line — producing a visible band. For quantitative metabolic results (not just positive/negative), a handheld reader interprets band intensity and converts it to a numerical value. That reader is the CAPEX element in our model.

What "metabolic risk detection" means here

This test is a risk flagging tool, not a clinical diagnostic. Primary biomarkers: HbA1c (chronic glycemic exposure — 3-month average blood sugar) and CRP (C-reactive protein — systemic inflammation and metabolic syndrome signal). HbA1c is validated at current TRL. CRP is 4–6 months from validation — deliberate sequencing, not a gap. The key clinical distinction: a positive result says "elevated risk — see a physician." It does not say "you have metabolic syndrome." That framing is both clinically correct and legally necessary pre-clearance.

Sensitivity and specificity

Sensitivity: correctly identifies people who have the risk condition (true positive rate). Specificity: correctly identifies people who don't (true negative rate). For a screening tool, sensitivity is prioritized — better to flag someone who turns out fine than miss someone who isn't. The ~200 patient validation likely shows preliminary numbers. Until formally published or COFEPRIS-reviewed, treat specific figures as internal data only — never cite them in public-facing patient materials.

TRL 8 — what it means and doesn't

TRL 8 = "System complete and qualified." Prototype functions. Lab validation done. ~200 patient evaluations show promising results. What TRL8 does NOT mean: regulatory clearance, peer-reviewed publication, scale manufacturing readiness, or commercial viability evidence. It means the science works. Every other question is a company-building question — and that's what this role is about.

COFEPRIS regulatory landscape — 2026

COFEPRIS regulates IVDs under the General Health Law. Lateral flow metabolic tests likely fall under Class II (moderate risk). Standard route: full technical dossier, clinical performance data, GMP evidence, Spanish labeling. Historical timeline: 12–24 months.

Critical 2025 update: COFEPRIS published the Abbreviated Regulatory Pathway (effective September 1, 2025), recognizing prior approvals from IMDRF and MDSAP member authorities — including FDA, CE Mark (MDR/IVDR), ANVISA, Health Canada, TGA, and others. Target review timeline under abbreviated route: 30 business days. That's a concrete acceleration lever if the device gets clearance in Brazil or the EU first.

The ANVISA–COFEPRIS MOU and what it actually means

In August 2025, ANVISA and COFEPRIS signed a formal MOU establishing a framework for regulatory cooperation and reliance. No formal mutual recognition exists yet — but COFEPRIS now explicitly accepts ANVISA approvals as reference under the abbreviated pathway.

This opens a concrete two-market strategy: obtain ANVISA clearance in Brazil first (well-understood IVD process, LATAM's largest diagnostic market), then use ANVISA as the reference document for an expedited COFEPRIS submission. Real-world timeline compression: from 18–24 months standalone Mexico path to potentially 9–15 months total via Brazil-first. Brazil also becomes a parallel commercial market, not just a regulatory stepping stone. Week-one action: RA consultant confirms ANVISA pathway eligibility for this specific device class.

Reader CAPEX — the full picture

A quantitative reader interprets band intensity and converts it to a numerical value — required if the device outputs a concentration number. If the device outputs a semi-quantitative risk tier (Low / Elevated / High), visual reading is sufficient and reader CAPEX disappears entirely. We assume a reader-based system given the quantitative validation methodology, but the model is more favorable without one. Reader cost: $80–200 USD at small volume. Pilot fleet at 100 sites: $15–20k USD. Model: owned CAPEX from seed round (Option A), transitioning to CDMO-subsidized placement when volume justifies the negotiation (Option C). The trigger for Option C is demonstrated volume — exactly what the pilot generates. Reader fleet is a ~10% line item in the seed round allocation, corrected to reflect 100-site scale.

Appendix A2 — Pilot Operations
What a pilot looks like.
The legal framing that makes pre-clearance pilots defensible

COFEPRIS regulates commercial sale and distribution of medical devices. It does not explicitly prohibit using an uncleared device in a controlled clinical performance evaluation — provided it's structured correctly. This is standard global practice: EU IVDR, FDA IDE pathway, and COFEPRIS's own framework all have equivalents. You are not selling a diagnostic service. You are evaluating the device's real-world performance as part of the evidence package required for eventual clearance.

The paper trail that protects you legally is identical to the paper trail that feeds your COFEPRIS submission. These are the same document set — building one builds both.

What you CAN do under clinical evaluation framing

✓ Operate with clinical oversight — a physician or licensed health professional must supervise or be formally present. TuDoc's teleconsult layer satisfies this structurally without additional hiring.

✓ Collect samples and run tests under a documented evaluation protocol with documented informed consent.

✓ Share results as informational, non-diagnostic findings — with mandatory language: "This is not a clinical diagnosis. Please consult a physician."

✓ Document everything — batch records, consent forms, result logs, adverse event tracking. Every piece feeds the COFEPRIS submission.

✓ Operate at zero cost to patient — cleanest legally, strongest for the TuDoc model. Cost absorbed as a member benefit or as evaluation operational cost.

What you CANNOT do — hard lines

✗ Issue a clinical diagnosis — result language must say "elevated risk," never "you have metabolic syndrome."

✗ Market as a cleared diagnostic product — no COFEPRIS seal, no equivalency claims in patient-facing materials.

✗ Scale commercially without clearance — 2 pilot sites under evaluation framing is defensible. 50 retail locations is commercial distribution.

✗ Make public sensitivity/specificity claims beyond what's in your formal evaluation protocol.

✗ Operate without a formal ethics/oversight framework — see below.

The five real risks — ranked

Risk 1 — COFEPRIS enforcement (High severity / Low-Med probability): Inspection determines you're running a commercial service without clearance. Mitigation: airtight clinical evaluation framing + documentation discipline.

Risk 2 — Channel partner liability exposure (High severity / Med probability): TuDoc or pharmacy legal asks "what's our liability if a result is wrong?" Mitigation: result language + indemnification clause in partnership agreement + digital delivery creates logged consent trail.

Risk 3 — Adverse event with no regulatory cover (High severity / Low probability): False negative → patient doesn't follow up → metabolic event. Mitigation: mandatory follow-up language on every result, clinical supervision, teleconsult CTA built into result delivery.

Risk 4 — Patient distress from false positive (Med severity / Med probability): Unnecessary medical anxiety and cost. Mitigation: trained staff who contextualize results, immediate teleconsult pathway.

Risk 5 — Pilot data rejected by COFEPRIS (Med severity / Low probability): Documentation doesn't meet technical requirements for Class II submission. Mitigation: RA consultant co-designs data collection protocol before the first test runs. Week-one investment, not optional.

Ethics committee vs. full IRB — what's actually required

Depends on where the pilot runs and how it's formally classified.

Hospital-based or formally classified clinical study: Requires full Comité de Ética en Investigación (CEI/IRB) — typically 3–6 months. Not compatible with a 90-day sprint.

Community-based clinical performance evaluation at non-hospital site (TuDoc, pharmacy): More flexibility. You still need a documented evaluation protocol and oversight structure — but this can be provided by an independent ethics consultant or through Tec de Monterrey's own research ethics infrastructure, which is a direct institutional asset from the spin-off's origin.

The Advisory Board can formally constitute an internal oversight committee for the evaluation protocol — provided one member has clinical research credentials and is designated as protocol officer. This is the lean path. It works if documented with the same rigor as a formal IRB. Not a shortcut — a structurally appropriate mechanism for this scale and setting.

Short version: Full IRB not required for TuDoc-model community evaluation. Formal ethics oversight document IS required. Advisory Board carries this. Flag as week-one action item alongside RA consultant engagement.

Results delivery

Digital — co-branded result via WhatsApp or in-app notification: Risk tier (Low / Moderate / Elevated) with mandatory framing: "This indicates elevated metabolic risk. This is not a clinical diagnosis." Direct deep-link to book a TuDoc teleconsult. Digital delivery does more than inform — it creates a timestamped record that the patient received the result and was directed to follow up, which is stronger liability documentation than paper. CTA click rate and teleconsult booking rate also feed directly into LQ2 behavioral data.

Secondary benefit: routes directly into TuDoc's platform and generates teleconsult bookings — pure revenue for them. Their incentive to push adoption to their member base is self-interested, not just contractual.

Paper fallback: For sites without digital infrastructure. Printed card with risk tier, pre-written guidance, and TuDoc booking CTA. Simple, auditable, zero tech dependency — lower behavioral data capture, used only where digital isn't viable.

Why TuDoc is the stronger pilot partner — incentive alignment

TuDoc's business model rewards utilization. A metabolic risk screening as a "member benefit preview" does three things for them: (1) increases perceived membership value at near-zero marginal cost, (2) generates teleconsult bookings that are pure platform revenue, (3) produces health outcome data that strengthens their clinical credibility narrative.

The zero-cost-to-patient model works perfectly here — test cost is shared or absorbed as a member benefit, blended into membership pricing. For TuDoc: differentiated offering + platform utilization. For us: clinical evaluation data + behavioral follow-through data + a motivated co-promoter with an existing, warm patient base.

Their incentive to push this to their members is real and self-interested — not just polite. That asymmetry is a GTM advantage you don't get in a pure pharmacy negotiation.

Digital delivery is actually stronger liability protection than paper — timestamped evidence the patient received the result and was directed to follow up, plus a CTA click log. The consent form signed before the test does significant work on top of that.
Brazil strategy
Does the Brazil-first regulatory path actually work — and what breaks it?
It works under one critical condition: the device classifies as Class II in Brazil. At Class II, ANVISA uses the Notificação route — simplified, no review queue, potentially months not years. Once notified, COFEPRIS's abbreviated pathway accepts ANVISA as reference authority, 30 business day target. Total: potentially 9 to 12 months dual-market vs. 18 to 24 standalone. What breaks it: Class III classification because of how quantitative metabolic claims are framed. The speed argument collapses entirely. Week-one action is an RA consultant confirming device class for this specific product and intended use. Everything downstream depends on that answer.
For a Brazil-native investor — why is this their story?
Because the ANVISA-COFEPRIS bridge inverts the sequence. This isn't a Mexico company adding Brazil in year three. It's a company that enters Brazil first as part of its clearance strategy, with Mexico following in 30 business days. A Brazil-native investor anchors in their home market and gets Mexico as a near-immediate follow-on rather than a speculative expansion. That's a home-market story, not an international bet.
Your pilot is in Mexico — does that validate the Brazil market?
Not directly. Brazil and Mexico have real structural differences — universal public healthcare through SUS, different pharmacy culture, different patient navigation. A Mexico pilot doesn't prove Brazil GTM. What it does prove is the behavioral hypothesis: that making metabolic risk visible at point of care changes what urban middle-class patients do next. That hypothesis travels. What doesn't travel is a copy-paste GTM — Brazil needs its own pilot and its own partners. What Series A capital funds is exactly that proof.
Commercial model
Why membership and primary care clinics — and not pharmacy chains or DTC?
Membership networks and primary care clinics give us three things pharmacies and DTC can't: a patient who already has a follow-up pathway, a partner whose business model rewards utilization, and a closed behavioral loop we can actually measure. Pharmacy volume is real — we use it for awareness — but the data trail ends at the door. DTC works when you have a brand and content engine. We have neither yet. When we have real patient stories and published results, DTC is a Phase 2 play.
What structural advantages does this test have over existing options?
The comparison isn't really against ELISA — it's against doing nothing, which is the actual competitor. ELISA is excellent for what it does: centralized lab, controlled conditions, full panel. It's also inaccessible to the population we're targeting. Our validation is benchmarked against ELISA as the reference standard — required by COFEPRIS and ANVISA. The clinical question isn't "is this as good as ELISA?" It's "is this good enough to flag elevated risk in a way that changes what the patient does next?" Those are different questions. ELISA is the destination. We're the access layer that routes people toward it.
Walk me through reader CAPEX in the seed round.
Pilot fleet is 4 readers across 2 sites at $80 to $200 USD each — $320 to $800 total, small. At 100 sites it's $15 to $20k in placed hardware — real working capital. The plan is owned CAPEX from seed now, then transition to CDMO-subsidized placement once demonstrated throughput justifies the negotiation. The pilot generates exactly that data. And if the device runs semi-quantitative visual mode, reader CAPEX is zero.
Capital & risk
Pilot margin is near zero — how do you survive 18 months on that?
The seed round funds the learning period explicitly. Margin turns at medium volume — the CDMO relationship and reader amortization are the levers, and both require scale we can only justify with pilot data. We're not projecting margin at pilot stage. We're earning the right to project it at the next investor conversation.
What would make you step back and reassess the entire model?
If the 90-day pilot shows that patients receive a risk result and consistently don't act on it within 14 days. That's the behavioral hypothesis the entire model depends on — not the technology, not the regulatory path, not the margin structure. If the test doesn't change what someone does next, we're building something interesting that doesn't create real health outcomes. I'd rather know that at week 12 than month 24.
How do you think about full-time transition?
Transition should be milestone-triggered, not calendar-triggered. First real traction signal — signed pilot agreement, committed channel partner, or seed close — is the right trigger. I'd want that explicit and agreed upfront. Ambiguity there creates misaligned expectations on both sides, and I'd rather surface that now than manage it under pressure.
